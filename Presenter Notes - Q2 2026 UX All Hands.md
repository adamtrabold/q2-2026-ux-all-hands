## Slide 2
the word for 2026 is evolution. It's no secret that the landscape is changing, and it's changing fast. You've certainly heard it in all the town halls and team meetings about the push to adopt AI tools in our workflows. It can be a bit overwhelming. It's easy to get distracted by the noise of social media, and the seemingly daily "step changes" in our jobs and industry. It can feel like we are always behind because maybe we don't see a tweet in the first 20 minutes it's out. And for good reason — we are a tech company. Not only that, but we're working within a specific tech environment — WordPress — that has its own needs and risks in an AI-enabled future. This isn't optional for us.

---

## Slide 3
And on top of it, these tools can be controversial, even in good faith. Things like energy and water use, ethicality of training data, and knock-on effects for our society long term are all ongoing industry discussions. We specifically have more base-level concerns — is it going to produce something good? Can I trust the data it's giving me? Does any of this make actual sense or is it just the Ai equivalent of meaningless corporate speak? It is okay to be skeptical.

---

## Slide 4
For those of you that really know me, you know I spend a lot of time outside work thinking about the paranormal...a world rife with simiilar skepticism. There are some distinctions there I think we can learn from here. Mainly that theres a difference between a **skeptic** and a **debunker**. If you'll humor me with an illustration...let's talk about bigfoot for a second.

---

## Slide 5 (True Believer focus)
A **true believer** may stop you on the street and deliver a fully rehearsed diatribe about how bigfoot is really an interdimensional ghost, a semi-corporeal, earth spirit thought form that can travel freely between many worlds. This, of course, explains why trails of footprints just stop in the middle of forests, how they cover so much ground, seemingly have a coherent large scale society, and yet, are still so elusive to catch sight of. They may even claim they've seen one themselves, been hit by a tossed rock, or maybe even fought one with their bare hands! Or maybe they've made friends in their wooded backyard, have a secret language together, and give them marshmallows. No matter what you say, their mind is not going to change. Bigfoot is real and among us.

---

## Slide 6 (Skeptic focus)
A **skeptic** would respond that serious claims require serious evidence — for much of this to be true would require a pretty significant rewrite of physics and our understanding about the natural world. But then again, theyd recognize there sure are some spooky instances of people seeing something that looks like a bigfoot disappearing into thin air! There is a deep history of electronic signals going haywire and weird mysterious lights during these sightings. And there are a lot of extremely large plaster feet casts that just don't make sense. Are there really that many people that would go to that effort just for a prank? It is really odd that we don't have any bodies...and people do love a good hoax. They might ask if the true believer has any photos, video, hair samples, etc. and continue the conversation.

---

## Slide 7 (Debunker focus)
A **debunker** would disregard it outright. They'd say that person was simply crazy, and move on with their day. Somewhere a unicorn would shed a single tear.

---

## Slide 8
We need to be more curious than the debunkers. It takes stepping forward to ride an adoption curve. That said, hype comes before the fall, and I believe we should remain generally in the skeptical camp in our day-to-day. We look out for lapses in judgement and logic, while also taking the opportunity here seriously. Yes, a lot of money is flowing into this space, but it is clearly not entirely hype at this point. These tools aren't new anymore.

---

## Slide 9
It may not feel like it, but you could have gotten an entire four-year degree in the time between now and when ChatGPT was released. Just Claude Code has now been out a year. Claude proper, 3. We are existing fully inside of a new context. For better or for worse, problem solving now looks very different compared to a few years ago. For the life of me I hope colleges have been focusing on principles rather than tools.

---

## Slide 10
But where do we sit in all of this? Some of us have been in this industry for multiple decades at this point, inside of multiple contexts. Just like Quark to InDesign, Omnigraffle to Miro, Photoshop to Figma...new tools mean new outputs mean new ways of working. It's on us to figure out how to make this shift while in motion. And, believe me, we are in motion.

---

## Slide 11
For all of UX's struggle to get a "seat at the table" the "modern product team" *has* had fairly well-defined roles for some time now. PMs are trained in uncovering business opportunity. Engineers are trained in making things that work. Researchers are trained in understanding people. Designers are trained making things desirable for those people. Has anything really changed here? Certainly not the core principles. I have a saw, but that doesn't make me a great cabinet maker.

---

## Slide 12
The reality is that it's not our responsibilities that have changed, but the smartest ways to spend our time has. And it will continue to shift.

---

## Slide 13 — ⭐ S.T.A.R. MOMENT
In truth, I believe we are positioned the best we have ever been if we can take advantage of this moment. If we were "cooked" every time someone said we were on twitter, we would all be working at Barnes and Noble already. The entire field of UX, our entire industry, was built with our hands tied behind our backs. Good with a capital G work has been an uphill battle since the beginning of the industry. Businesses do not inherently understand the gaps between them and their customers. Research and Design are often viewed as expenses, not value creators. You can reasonably guess how much your MRR will go up with a new sales hire. You can't do that with our roles. And yet, we exist within and have created a particular environment where our work is deeply valued and drives decisions. To borrow the cooked metaphor — if anything, it's been like being a head chef forced to communicate to their staff, create the menu, and grow the business entirely via Instagram posts. We are not cooked — we can **finally** cook!

---

## Slide 14
This new toolset is great at two things — **automation** and **assistance**. The trick is matching the task to the mindset.

---

## Slide 15
Certain parts of our work can certainly be **automated** more easily than others. This enables deeper focus on the work only we can do. Examples of **automation** — quick prototyping — no more faking data, generating specs, dragging arrows and setting the same animations across 100 screens; analysis against defined standards eg design system and content rules, letting it do the grunt work of creating components and exporting; quick drafts of repetitive messaging eg for recruiting or templated content

---

## Slide 16
Certain parts of our work will benefit from **assistance**. This enables better quality of work. Deeper understanding and thought, more thorough exploration, stronger outputs. Examples of **assistance** — quickly iterating a bunch of ideas closer to the real outputs than ever; pressure testing strategies and finding quotes from interviews; learning about domains, systems, context, and prepping for conversations with different roles and mindsets

---

## Slide 17
"Best practices" are in progress, there are certainly more level ups to uncover. But we have to remember that what we are individually accountable for does not go away just because the toolkit has changed. Researchers are accountable to driving customer and product understanding that shapes direction and increases the credibility of our decisions. Designers are accountable to our products serving those user needs so well people will pay gobs of money to use them.

---

## Slide 18
And our customers — they are *also* using these tools...as well as *creating* experiences leveraging them. We have to hero them both succeed in this moment in their particular roles, but also in making the next generation of experiences online. Our tools exist in the dead center of the most technical and least technical spaces in our industry. We have to understand both sides of that equation, and this means we have to get closer than ever to the technical underpinnings of what we do. We need to understand, more than ever before, how things work. Our user's goals AND the architecture of the product and its implications. Like other makers before us, our level of success will be directly correlated with our ability to build our understanding. We have to understand AI's impact on our users' behavior, mental models, expectations, etc.

---

## Slide 19
This evolution is energizing and exciting, but everything good is hard. Wasted time is still wasted time, and we can move a million miles an hour in the wrong direction easier than ever. Hop into Figma, make some mocks, iterate and refine and send to dev...these are established workflows. What does the process look like when an entire flow can be created in a prompt, without the helpful canvas view to visualize things against each other? We have to explore to figure out what works. What does it take for designers to iterate with better context and prototype more robustly and easier at the same time? For researchers to get even better data, deliver even more effective insights, and go even deeper into driving decision making? AI can't tell you why, can't understand context, can't distinguish signal from noise. Walls will blur across roles — we will see PMs and Devs creating artifacts we may have been the only ones behind in the past — but our particular expertise, paired with tighter learning loops in a newly more collaborative environment will afford *better* outcomes if we are intentional. Muscle building is hard and takes time, but I'm excited for the collaboration and environment of trust it will build with our peers. We could consider this a forcing function to better relationships.

---

## Slide 20
And we know this is hard. We are all busy. It's a struggle to find time to learn when there are projects left and right. As we've said before — block time for this stuff.  Build in extra time in your projects to account for it. We heavily encourage you to make the time to learn these new tools. Work with your manager on prioritization against active work. Are there projects you could try a new way of working as part of your day to day? Where a gem or a skill would really help? I think our org initiatives and exploratory work are perfect places to experiment and adopt more fully later. Are there low-risk exploration projects with your teams that aren't on a hard timeline? Think back to the categories — automation and assistance. Are there places you're doing repetitive work? Are there places where you find yourself "spinning your wheels?" Constrained? These are ideal places to think about where an AI tool might help you. You have our support to spend this time, and to prioritize this learning, but I recognize that's abstract. Keep tight with your manager and work together to ideate.

---

## Slide 21
The reality is that what we do every day and how we do it is changing and will continue to change. This is an industry shift, not a company one. I strongly believe the future will be for the makers. The people pushing past their "station" and caring deeply about the whole process of making great things. There is no reason in the age of AI to not be more passionate than ever about what goes out the door.

---

## Slide 22
We can look at other teams behind iconic work for inspiration — Ray and Charles Eames becoming experts in plywood and fiberglass fabrication to innovate the simple chair, Apple's industrial design team becoming experts in magnets to create an elegant laptop open/close mechanism, Ray Kraft becoming an expert in pasteurization so that he can leverage thousands of pounds of gifted surplus government cheese to create the greatest processed food vertical of all time, Kraft Mac & Cheese. Ok jury's out on that last one, but we **have** to grow in the knowledge and understanding behind what we put out into the world.

---

## Slide 23
This isn't about getting things out the door faster (though that is a possible side effect). It's more importantly about being *more nimble* — deliberate as well as efficient. Less Rhino, more Spider-Man. The future is for those who believe a faster horse affords shorter journeys AND better destinations. Those energized by experimentation, and who take accountability by default - because they can't imagine not having a voice, not because they are forced to have it.
